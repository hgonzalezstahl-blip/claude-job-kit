# Resume Playbook — What Lands Interviews

> Pitch reads this before producing every resume. These are the rules that separate top-1% applications from the generic stack.

---

## THE TOP-1% PRINCIPLE

Most strong candidates send one resume to many roles. Top-1% candidates send one resume to one role, every time. Tailoring is the entire game. The keyword overlap, the surfaced achievements, the language calibration — they all change per application.

If you're tempted to "reuse the last one," don't. The cost of 30 minutes of tailoring is one shot at a real interview pipeline.

---

## ACHIEVEMENT FRAMING

Every bullet on the resume describes an **achievement**, not a responsibility. The difference:

| Responsibility (weak) | Achievement (strong) |
|---|---|
| Responsible for managing inventory across multiple locations | Reduced inventory holding cost by $2.3M across 47 sites by redesigning reorder thresholds |
| Worked on AI integration projects | Led AI-enabled invoice processing rollout that cut manual entry by 78% and removed 12 FTE from the back-office loop |
| Helped develop process improvements | Drove 6 lean initiatives across procurement, three of which became standard procedure across the BU |

The pattern that wins:

**[Strong action verb] + [what was done specifically] + [quantified or named outcome] + (optional: business value)**

### Action verbs that land

Use these. They imply ownership and outcome:

- Built, designed, architected, engineered, developed, launched, shipped
- Led, ran, owned, directed, ran point on, managed end-to-end
- Reduced, cut, eliminated, removed, accelerated, compressed
- Increased, grew, scaled, expanded, doubled, tripled
- Negotiated, closed, secured, won, landed
- Diagnosed, investigated, identified, root-caused
- Migrated, consolidated, restructured, redesigned, rebuilt
- Trained, coached, mentored, hired
- Standardized, codified, documented, productized

### Action verbs to AVOID (corporate-speak / AI-smell)

These are nearly universal AI-resume tells. Replace them with verbs from the list above:

- **Spearheaded** — overused, vague
- **Championed** — implies advocacy, not action
- **Orchestrated** — usually a synonym for "managed", say what you did
- **Leveraged** — almost always means "used", just say "used"
- **Harnessed** — same problem as leveraged
- **Drove** (alone, without a metric) — fine when followed by a number, weak when standalone
- **Facilitated** — usually means "did nothing concrete", surface the actual action
- **Empowered** — empty
- **Optimized** (without metric) — quantify or replace
- **Streamlined** (without metric) — same
- **Synergized** — never use
- **Curated** — only when literally curating (content, collections); not for projects

### Quantification is the differentiator

If you can't quantify with a number, quantify with **scale or named system**:

- "Across 47 sites" / "Across 12 markets" / "For 3,400 daily users"
- "On a $14M annual book" / "Processing $2.1B in transaction volume"
- "Reducing cycle time from 14 days to 3 days"
- "First in the business unit to deploy" / "Adopted by all 6 sister teams within 6 months"

If a real metric isn't in the master CV, ask the user for it before drafting. Don't guess.

## RESUME ANATOMY

### Header
Name (large, bold). Then a single line: City, State | Email | Phone | LinkedIn URL. No headshot, no graphics, no "available immediately" notes.

### Summary / Profile (3–4 lines)
**Tailored to the specific role.** The opening sentence names the target role, the second names the impact-bearing skill set, the third names the strongest evidence, the fourth (optional) names a unique angle (bilingual, cross-industry, M.S. in progress, etc.).

**Avoid:** "Results-driven professional with X years of experience." Cut it.

### Experience
Reverse chronological. For each role:

```
Company Name City, State
Title Mon YYYY – Mon YYYY
[3–6 achievement bullets]
```

**Bullet count guidance:**
- Most recent / most relevant role: 5–6 bullets
- Older / less relevant role: 2–3 bullets
- Total bullets across all roles: 12–18 for a 1-page resume, 18–25 for 2 pages

### Education
Degree, institution, graduation year. Honors only if 3.5+ GPA or top-tier institution recognition.

### Skills (when relevant)
Group by category (Tools, Methodologies, Domain). Don't list things any literate adult has (Microsoft Word, email). Do list things the JD specifically mentions, in the JD's exact phrasing.

### Certifications, Languages, Other
Only if relevant or differentiating.

## ATS RULES (NON-NEGOTIABLE)

Applicant Tracking Systems parse text. They do not parse:

- ❌ Tables (parsed as random word soup)
- ❌ Text boxes
- ❌ Header / footer text (often dropped)
- ❌ Graphics, logos, headshots
- ❌ Skill-rating bars / star ratings
- ❌ Multiple columns (some ATS read across rows, mixing column content)
- ❌ Exotic fonts (stick to Calibri, Arial, Helvetica, Times New Roman, Georgia, Garamond)
- ❌ PDFs created from non-text sources (e.g., scanned PDFs)

ATS requires:

- ✅ Single-column layout
- ✅ Standard section headings: SUMMARY, EXPERIENCE, EDUCATION, SKILLS, CERTIFICATIONS
- ✅ Dates in consistent format: "Jan 2023 – Present" or "01/2023 – Present"
- ✅ .docx file (most universally parseable)
- ✅ Plain bullet points (• or -)
- ✅ No special characters in section names

**Keyword strategy:**
- Pull exact phrases from the JD (not synonyms — "demand planning" if they wrote "demand planning", not "supply forecasting")
- Use the keywords in context, in achievement bullets, where they naturally describe what was done
- Aim to cover 70–80% of the JD's must-have keywords. 100% feels stuffed; under 50% gets filtered

## LENGTH

- 0–10 years experience: 1 page (hard rule)
- 10–20 years experience: 1–2 pages
- 20+ years OR senior leadership / academic: 2 pages

Default to the page count that matches the user's years of experience per the rule above. For candidates under 10 years, default to **1 page**, and allow 2 only when the role demands depth (senior IC, lead, manager) and the bullets earn the second page.

---

## TAILORING CHECKLIST

For every application, run through this:

- [ ] Job title in the resume summary matches or echoes the JD's title
- [ ] Top 3 must-have keywords from JD appear in the first half of the resume
- [ ] Bullets in most recent role lead with the skills the JD weights most
- [ ] Older roles trimmed if not relevant; expanded if highly relevant
- [ ] Education / certifications surfaced only if they map to JD requirements
- [ ] Skills section reordered so JD-relevant categories come first
- [ ] Profile rewritten — never reused verbatim from prior application

## RESUME-SPECIFIC AI/CORPORATE-SPEAK TO SCRUB

Beyond the verbs above, watch for these phrases. Hiring managers see them on every resume and skim past:

- "Results-driven professional"
- "Detail-oriented" (unless backed by specific evidence)
- "Strong communicator" (show, don't tell)
- "Team player"
- "Self-starter"
- "Proven track record" (the rest of the resume IS the proven track record)
- "Excellent interpersonal skills"
- "Highly motivated"
- "Passionate about" (unless tied to a specific domain with evidence)
- "Out-of-the-box thinker"
- "Synergistic"
- "Best-of-breed"
- "Cutting-edge", "state-of-the-art", "world-class"
- "Game-changing"
- "Robust", "scalable" (when not actually about systems)
- "Cross-functional collaboration" alone (name the functions: "with engineering, finance, and ops")
- "Mission-critical"

If a sentence still works after deleting these phrases, delete them.

## COVER LETTER PRINCIPLES

When the user wants a cover letter, follow these:

### Length
250–350 words. Never longer. Hiring managers spend ~30 seconds on a cover letter — write for that scan.

### Structure
1. **Opener (2–3 sentences).** Reference something specific the company has done — a product launch, a public statement, a recent investment, a strategy shift. Not "I'm excited to apply" / "I am writing to express my interest." Open with substance.
2. **Strongest pitch (1 paragraph).** The 1–2 specific accomplishments from the user's history that most directly map to what the JD spends the most words on. Quantified.
3. **Fit angle (1 paragraph).** Why the user is the right person for this *specific* environment, not just any company doing similar work. Reference culture, scale, or stage signals from the JD.
4. **Direct close (1–2 sentences).** What the user wants next: a conversation. No "Looking forward to hearing from you" — instead, "I'd like to discuss how my [X experience] could fit your [Y need]. Available [next week]."

### Voice
- First person ("I"), confident, plain English
- Same anti-corporate-speak rules as the resume — no "leverage", "spearhead", "passionate about"
- Concrete details over generalities
- One personal note is fine (bilingual, cross-industry pivot, etc.) if it serves the pitch

### Avoid
- Restating the resume bullet by bullet
- Generic flattery ("Your company is a leader in…")
- Salary discussion
- Apology framing ("Although I don't have direct experience in X, I…")
- Anything over a single page

## INTERVIEW-PROBABILITY MULTIPLIERS BEYOND THE RESUME

Pitch should remind the user of these when relevant. They matter more than any single resume edit:

1. **Internal referrals.** A referred candidate is 4–14× more likely to be interviewed (industry data varies). Before sending a cold application, check LinkedIn for first or second-degree connections at the target company.
2. **Reach out directly.** Find the hiring manager or recruiter on LinkedIn after applying. A short, specific note (referencing the role and one concrete reason for fit) lifts response rates noticeably.
3. **Apply within 48 hours of posting.** Volume of applicants spikes after week one; recruiters source from the early stack first.
4. **Match the platform's expectations.** LinkedIn Easy Apply, Workday, Greenhouse, Ashby, Lever — each has different fields. Fill them all, even the optional ones.
5. **Quality of LinkedIn matters as much as the resume.** Recruiters source from LinkedIn before they read resumes. A LinkedIn profile that mirrors the resume's tailoring (or at least covers the same keywords) compounds the signal.

These are not Pitch's job to execute, but Pitch should call them out in the fit summary when they apply.
